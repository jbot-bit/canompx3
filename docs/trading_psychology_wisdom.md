# Trading Psychology Wisdom -- AI Coach Knowledge Base

Extracted from three foundational texts. Psychology principles, behavioral patterns detectable
from trade data, key quotes with page references, and lessons about discipline under drawdown.

---

## BOOK 1: Market Wizards (Schwager, 2012)

### Source: Dr. Van K. Tharp -- "The Psychology of Trading" (p.196-210)

#### The Three Clusters of Trading Success

Van Tharp's Investment Psychology Inventory identifies three clusters that correlate with
trading success (p.197):

1. **Psychological Factor** -- well-rounded personal life, positive attitude, motivation to make
   money, lack of internal conflict, responsibility for results.
2. **Management and Discipline Factor** -- risk control, patience, intuition.
3. **Decision-Making Factor** -- technical knowledge, sound judgment free of cognitive biases,
   independent thinking.

#### Profile of the Losing Trader (p.197-198)

> "The composite profile of a losing trader would be someone who is highly stressed and has
> little protection from stress, has a negative outlook on life and expects the worst, has a lot
> of conflict in his/her personality, and blames others when things go wrong."

**Named Principle: RESPONSIBILITY FIRST.** Losing traders externalize blame. Winners own results.

#### Profile of the Winning Trader (p.198)

> "The winning trader is someone who is a mature, well-rounded, organized, serious about
> trading, willing to let the market determine their actions, and has a positive outlook on life
> and trading."

#### The Two Forms of Stress (p.199)

1. **Worry** -- consumes decision-making capacity. "Our brains have a limited capacity for
   processing information. If your mind is preoccupied with worry, that worry takes up most of
   the decision space."
2. **Fight/Flight Response** -- causes traders to (a) narrow focus, (b) revert to beginner
   behaviors, (c) expend more energy on fewer alternatives, (d) follow the crowd.

**Named Principle: STRESS NARROWS.** Under stress, traders default to doing what they were
doing, only harder. "Putting more energy into trading decisions does not help you make more
money. Instead you will tend to make quick, irrational choices."

**Detectable Pattern:** Increased trade frequency after losses. Position sizing that stays
constant or increases during drawdowns rather than being cut.

#### Van Tharp's Top Trader Traits (p.200-203)

1. **They have a tested positive-expectancy system.** They know it works. Confidence in the
   system prevents discretionary overrides.
2. **They use systems that suit their personality.** Mismatch between trader psychology and
   system style causes abandonment during drawdowns.
3. **They totally accept responsibility.** "When top traders make mistakes, they don't make
   excuses, blame someone or something else, or wallow in negative emotion."
4. **They understand the big picture.** They know the statistical distribution of their trades
   and don't overweight any single outcome.
5. **They are not trying to outsmart the market.** They follow their system. Period.

#### Common Biases of Losing Traders (p.201-203)

- **Gambler's Fallacy:** Believing a string of losses makes a win more likely.
- **Limiting Profitable Trades:** Taking profits too early while letting losses run.
- **Not Pulling the Trigger:** Fear of loss prevents entry. Most severe after a drawdown.
- **Focusing on Price Instead of Market Structure.**

**Detectable Pattern: THE TRIGGER FREEZE.** After a series of losses, traders skip valid signals.
This is measurable: compare signal generation to actual entries. Gaps increase after drawdowns.

#### Van Tharp's Key Insight on System-Trader Mismatch (p.204)

> "A lot of people come up with a set of rules that will work beautifully. But they lose money
> because the system doesn't fit them. They can't follow the rules."

**Named Principle: SYSTEM-PERSONALITY FIT.** The best system you won't follow is worse than a
mediocre system you will follow consistently.

### Source: Ed Seykota (p.76-87)

#### Core Psychology Philosophy

> "Everybody gets what they want out of the market." (p.76, chapter title)

> "Win or lose, everybody gets what they want out of the market. Some people seem to like to
> lose, so they win by losing money." (p.82)

**Named Principle: THE WANT BENEATH THE WANT.** Self-sabotage is not random. Traders who
consistently violate their own rules are getting something from the violation (excitement,
self-punishment, validation of unworthiness). Until the underlying want changes, the behavior
won't change.

#### Seykota on Losing

> "There are old traders and there are bold traders, but there are very few old, bold traders."
> (p.83)

> "The elements of good trading are: (1) cutting losses, (2) cutting losses, and (3) cutting
> losses. If you can follow these three rules, you may have a chance." (p.82)

#### Seykota on Emotional Trading

> "If you want to know everything about the market, go to the beach. Push and pull your hands
> with the waves. Some are bigger waves, some are smaller. But if you try to push the wave out
> when it's coming in, it'll never work. The market is always right." (p.84)

> "I think that if people look deeply enough into their trading patterns, they find that, on
> balance, including all their goals, they are getting what they want, even though they may not
> understand it or want to admit it." (p.85)

**Named Principle: THE MARKET IS ALWAYS RIGHT.** Fighting the market is fighting reality. Your
opinion about where price should be is irrelevant. Price is where price is.

#### Seykota on Risk

> "Risk no more than you can afford to lose, and also risk enough so that a win is meaningful."
> (p.84)

**Named Principle: MEANINGFUL RISK.** Too little risk = no emotional engagement = sloppy
execution. Too much risk = fear-driven decisions = system abandonment. The sweet spot is where
a loss stings but doesn't cripple.

### Source: Paul Tudor Jones (p.60-70)

#### The Cathartic Cotton Trade (p.62-64)

Jones lost 60-70% of his accounts' equity in a single cotton trade. This was his turning point.

> "That was when I first decided I had to learn discipline and money management. It was a
> cathartic experience for me, in the sense that I went to the edge, questioned my very ability
> as a trader, and decided that I was not going to quit. I was determined to come back and
> fight." (p.63)

**Named Principle: THE CRUCIBLE.** Nearly every great trader has a near-death trading experience
that transforms their approach to risk. The lesson is always the same: position sizing discipline.

#### Jones's Defense-First Mindset (p.64)

> "Now I spend my day trying to make myself as happy and relaxed as I can be. If I have
> positions going against me, I get right out; if they are going for me, I keep them." (p.64)

> "I am always thinking about losing money as opposed to making money. Back then, in that
> cotton trade, I had a vision of July going to 89 cents and I thought about all the money I was
> going to make on 400 contracts. I didn't think about what I could lose." (p.64)

**Named Principle: DEFENSE FIRST.** Elite traders think about downside before upside. Beginners
fantasize about profits. The mental shift from "how much can I make" to "how much can I lose"
is the defining psychological transition.

**Detectable Pattern:** Review whether a trader's journal/notes reference targets more than
stops. A defense-first trader discusses stop levels and risk before profit targets.

#### Jones on Monthly Discipline (p.64)

> "Right now I am down about 6.5 percent for the month. I have a 3.5 percent stop on my equity
> for the rest of the month. I want to make sure that I never have a double-digit loss in any
> month." (p.64)

**Named Principle: MONTHLY CIRCUIT BREAKER.** Hard caps on periodic losses prevent emotional
spiraling. When you hit the cap, you stop trading. No exceptions.

**Detectable Pattern:** Monthly P&L drawdown tracking. Flag months where losses exceed a
predefined threshold as circuit-breaker violations.

#### Jones on Flexibility (p.60-61)

Schwager's observation about Jones:

> "This 180-degree shift in opinion within a short time span exemplified the extreme flexibility
> that underlies Jones' trading success. He not only quickly abandoned his original position, but
> was willing to join the other side once the evidence indicated his initial projection was
> wrong." (p.60)

**Named Principle: INTELLECTUAL FLEXIBILITY.** The ability to reverse a position (psychologically,
not just mechanically) is rare. Most traders anchor to their original thesis. Jones changes his
mind when the market tells him he's wrong, without ego damage.

#### Jones on Persistence (p.65)

> "Until I change my mind, fundamentally. Otherwise I will keep cutting my position size down
> as I have more losing trades." (On how many times he'll try to pick a turning point)

**Named Principle: SCALED CONVICTION.** If the thesis still holds, keep trying but with smaller
size. Each failed attempt reduces position, never increases it. This is the opposite of revenge
trading (where size increases after losses).

**Detectable Pattern:** After consecutive losses on the same thesis, does position size decrease
(disciplined) or increase (revenge trading)?

### Source: Marty Schwartz (p.125-137)

#### The Marine Analogy (p.126)

> "The rigorous Marine training gave me the confidence to believe that I could perform at levels
> beyond my previous expectations." (p.126)

Schwartz credits Marine training with building the mental toughness required for trading.
The key parallel: trading requires operating under extreme pressure while following rules.

#### Schwartz on the Analyst-to-Trader Transformation (p.128-130)

Schwartz spent years as a securities analyst losing money trading on fundamentals before
switching to technical analysis and becoming consistently profitable.

> "I always did my own analysis. But I went through a metamorphosis when I turned from a
> securities analyst to a speculator." (p.128)

> "The key thing that changed was when I learned to separate my ego needs from making money.
> When I was able to accept being wrong. Before, admitting I was wrong was more upsetting than
> losing the money." (p.130)

**Named Principle: EGO SEPARATION.** The need to be right is the most expensive emotion in
trading. Schwartz only became profitable when he could be wrong without identity damage.

**Detectable Pattern:** Holding losers past stop levels. Average losing trade duration compared
to average winning trade duration -- if losers are held longer, ego attachment is present.

#### Schwartz on Knowing When You're Wrong (p.131)

> "I have learned one thing about getting out when I am wrong. I have my antenna up all the
> time, and if anything doesn't feel right, I get out. I trust my instincts." (p.131)

#### Schwartz on Recovery After Losses (p.133)

> "Whenever I have had a bad losing streak, I have been able to pull back, lick my wounds, and
> come back. That ability to withdraw and regroup has been critical." (p.133)

**Named Principle: TACTICAL WITHDRAWAL.** After significant losses, reduce size dramatically.
The goal shifts from making money to rebuilding confidence. Only scale back up after a string
of small wins.

**Detectable Pattern:** After a drawdown exceeding X%, does the trader reduce position size for
the next N trades? If not, they're not practicing tactical withdrawal.

### Source: Bruce Kovner (p.31-46)

#### Kovner on Predetermined Stops (p.37)

> "Whenever I enter a position, I have a predetermined stop. That is the only way I can sleep.
> I know where I'm getting out before I get in. The position size on a trade is determined by the
> stop, and the stop is determined on a technical basis." (p.37)

**Named Principle: SLEEP TEST.** If a position prevents sleep, it's too large. The stop must be
defined before entry, and position size must be derived from the stop distance.

#### Kovner on the Soybean Lesson (p.32-34)

Kovner's third trade turned $4,000 into $45,000 in soybeans, but he nearly gave it all back:

> "At one point I had a $45,000 profit... I watched my profit go from $45,000 to about $22,000
> in a single day. I was in agony. I said to myself, 'I can't take this anymore.'" (p.33-34)

He was so shaken he couldn't trade for a week. But the lesson was permanent:

> "The point is that from that day on, I never allowed a large gain to turn into a large loss."

**Named Principle: THE PROFIT GIVEBACK WALL.** Set a trailing threshold on open profits. Once
a trade has moved significantly in your favor, never let it return to breakeven. The
psychological pain of turning a winner into a loser is more damaging than taking a small loss.

#### Kovner on Risk of Ruin (p.38)

> "The first rule of trading -- there are probably many first rules -- is don't get caught in a
> situation in which you can lose a great deal of money for reasons you don't understand." (p.38)

> "Whenever I have had a loss I have been able to say to myself, 'Well, I can figure out the
> reason why I lost money.' I don't want to ever have a situation where I lose money and I can't
> explain it." (p.38)

**Named Principle: EXPLAINABLE LOSSES ONLY.** Every loss should be traceable to a specific,
understood reason. Unexplainable losses indicate position sizing beyond your understanding of
the instrument, or hidden correlated risks.

#### Kovner on Emotional Detachment (p.42)

> "Michael Marcus taught me one other thing that is absolutely critical: You have to be willing
> to make mistakes regularly; there is nothing wrong with it. Michael taught me about making
> your best judgment, being wrong, making your next best judgment, being wrong, making your
> third best judgment, and then doubling your money." (p.42)

**Named Principle: SERIAL BEST JUDGMENT.** Trading is an iterative process of making your best
call, being wrong, and making another call. The trader who cannot be wrong serially will never
capture the trade that makes the year.

### Source: Larry Hite (p.87-94)

#### The 1% Rule (p.90)

> "The very first rule we live by at Mint is: Never risk more than 1 percent of total equity on
> any trade. By only risking 1 percent, I am indifferent to any individual trade." (p.90)

**Named Principle: THE INDIFFERENCE THRESHOLD.** Risk per trade should be small enough that
the outcome of any single trade is emotionally irrelevant. Indifference to individual outcomes
is the prerequisite for system adherence.

**Detectable Pattern:** Calculate risk-per-trade as % of equity. If it exceeds 2%, the trader is
likely to experience emotional interference.

#### Good Bets vs. Winning Bets (p.90)

> "There are really four kinds of trades or bets: good bets, bad bets, winning bets, and losing
> bets. Most people think that a losing trade was a bad bet. That is absolutely wrong. You can
> lose money even on a good bet." (p.90)

**Named Principle: BET QUALITY INDEPENDENCE.** The quality of a trade decision is independent
of its outcome. A disciplined entry that follows the system and loses is a GOOD trade. A random
entry that ignores the system and wins is a BAD trade. Judge process, not results.

**Detectable Pattern:** Review trades that violated system rules but were profitable. These are
the most dangerous -- they reinforce bad habits. Flag "lucky wins" that were system violations.

#### Hite on Why People Don't Follow Systems (p.88)

> "Some will alter their system or jump from system to system as each one has a losing period.
> Others will be unable to resist second-guessing the trading signals. Whenever I go to a money
> management conference... I always hear the same story: 'My system worked great, but I just
> didn't take the gold trade, and that would have been my biggest winner.'" (p.88)

**Named Principle: THE SKIPPED TRADE.** The biggest winner is almost always the trade you
didn't take. This is because the trades that feel most uncomfortable (biggest breakouts after
long consolidations) are psychologically the hardest to enter but have the highest expectancy.

**Detectable Pattern:** Compare system signals to actual entries. Calculate the P&L of skipped
signals separately. If skipped trades outperform taken trades, the trader is filtering by
emotion rather than system.

#### Hite on Why This Works (p.88)

> "There is a very important message here: People don't change. That is why this whole game
> works." (p.88)

## BOOK 2: Way of the Turtle (Faith, 2007)

### The Eight Cognitive Biases That Destroy Traders (p.41-47)

Curtis Faith provides the clearest taxonomy of cognitive biases affecting trading. Each has a
detectable behavioral signature:

| Bias | Definition | Trading Manifestation | Detectable Pattern |
|------|------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **Loss Aversion** | Losses feel 2x as painful as equivalent gains feel good | Holding losers, cutting winners | Avg loss duration > avg win duration |
| **Sunk Cost Effect** | Treating spent money as more valuable | "I can't sell now, I'm already down $X" | Increasing hold time as loss grows |
| **Disposition Effect** | Selling winners, holding losers | Locking in small gains while riding large losses | Win/loss size ratio < 1.0 when system expects > 1.0 |
| **Outcome Bias** | Judging decisions by outcomes | Abandoning good system after losing streak | System changes after 3-5 consecutive losses |
| **Recency Bias** | Overweighting recent events | Skipping trades in markets that recently lost money | Entry rate drops after recent losses in a market |
| **Anchoring** | Fixating on reference prices | "I'll sell when it gets back to my entry" | Clustering of exits near entry prices |
| **Bandwagon Effect** | Believing what others believe | Piling into popular trades late | Entry timing correlated with crowd sentiment peaks |
| **Law of Small Numbers** | Drawing conclusions from tiny samples | "It worked 4 out of 6 times, it must be good" | Strategy changes after < 30 trades |

### The Cocoa Lesson -- Recency Bias in Action (p.76-77)

Faith taught a friend the Turtle system. The friend skipped a massive winning cocoa short
because he'd had 17 consecutive losing trades in cocoa before the big move.

> "There are 17 losing trades in a row in the cocoa market before a very sizable winning trade
> that started in November 1998." (p.76)

The friend's recency bias caused him to skip the one trade that would have made his year.

**Named Principle: THE 17-LOSS THRESHOLD.** In trend-following and breakout systems, the
biggest winners often come after the longest strings of losers. The system that feels most
broken is often about to pay off. This is structurally identical to ORB trading during low-
volatility consolidation periods.

**Detectable Pattern:** Track consecutive losses per instrument. If the trader reduces entries
after N consecutive losses (while the system keeps generating signals), recency bias is active.

### Think Like a Turtle -- Core Principles (p.74-88)

#### Principle 1: It's Not About Being Right (p.74-75)

> "Turtles do not care about being right. They care about making money." (p.74)

> "If your trades are losers most of the time, that shows that you are not trying to predict the
> future." (p.74)

> "If you have 10 losing trades in a row and you are sticking to your plan, you are trading well;
> you are just having a bit of bad luck." (p.75)

**Named Principle: RIGHTNESS IS IRRELEVANT.** Win rate is not a measure of trading quality.
System adherence is the measure. A trader who takes 10 consecutive losses while following the
system is performing perfectly.

#### Principle 2: Forget the Past (p.75-76)

> "Turtles learn from the past but don't worry about it. They don't berate themselves for
> mistakes they have made. They also don't criticize themselves for trades in which they lost
> money; they know that is part of the game." (p.75)

**Named Principle: PROCESS AMNESIA.** After each trade, reset emotionally. The previous trade's
outcome has zero predictive value for the next trade. Post-loss rumination is the direct cause
of the Trigger Freeze and the Skipped Trade.

#### Principle 3: Think in Probabilities (p.78-86)

> "Trading is much the same as insuring against uncertain risks... The best you can do is be
> confident that the rewards will outweigh the risks over the long run." (p.79)

Faith shows the R-multiple distribution of Turtle trades: 3,746 losing trades vs 1,854 winning
trades, but the winners include 43 trades returning 10R or more (p.85). The math works
despite a 67% loss rate.

**Named Principle: THE INSURANCE MODEL.** You are a trading actuary. Each trade is a policy.
Some policies pay out (losses), but the premiums collected across the portfolio (winners)
exceed the payouts. No insurance company panics over a single claim.

#### Principle 4: Take Responsibility (p.87-88)

> "The bottom line is that you make the trades and you are responsible for the outcome. Don't
> blame anyone else for giving you bad advice or withholding secrets from you." (p.88)

> "Blaming others for your mistakes is a sure way to lose." (p.88)

### Mastering Your Demons (p.250-259)

#### Ego: The Primary Demon (p.250-251)

> "Our egos drive us to believe that we possess some kind of special knowledge to prove to
> ourselves that we are somehow superior to others. Our egos don't want us to limit ourselves to
> commonly known truths. The ego wants secrets." (p.250)

> "The ego wants to be right, it wants to predict, and it wants to know secrets." (p.251)

**Named Principle: THE EGO TRAP.** Complexity satisfies ego; simplicity makes money. Traders
gravitate toward complex systems because simple rules feel insufficiently special. The Turtle
rules were simple enough to write on an index card, yet most Turtles couldn't follow them.

> "Some of us thought that trading successfully couldn't possibly be that simple; that there must
> be something else to it." (p.250)

#### The Ping-Pong Parable (p.252-255)

Faith won a Turtle Ping-Pong tournament against a vastly superior player because the better
player's ego made the outcome too important. The same Turtle who lost the tournament also
failed at trading, and blamed Rich Dennis for "withholding secrets."

> "His ego was too tied up in his own trading for him to be able to see that the reasons for his
> poor performance were inside him." (p.254)

**Named Principle: OUTCOME ATTACHMENT.** When winning matters too much, performance
degrades. The trader who NEEDS this trade to work will hold losers, cut winners, and override
the system. Paradoxically, caring less about individual outcomes produces better aggregate
results.

#### Humility as Edge (p.255-256)

> "Humility allows you to accept the future as something that is unknowable. Humility will keep
> you from trying to make predictions. Humility will keep you from taking it personally when a
> trade goes against you." (p.255)

#### The Money Attachment Problem (p.267-268)

> "I think it is easier to make a lot of money if you don't really want it badly." (p.267)

> "One of the Turtles was strongly affected by the large fluctuations in equity... Making a lot of
> money was very important to him. At one point I returned from vacation and found out that he
> had destroyed his telephone because he was so angry that the market had moved against us."
> (p.267)

**Named Principle: THE MONEY PARADOX.** Traders who care most about money perform worst.
Money anxiety tightens decision-making, triggers loss aversion, and causes system abandonment
during drawdowns. The correct focus is on process quality, not P&L.

**Detectable Pattern:** Correlation between account equity and system adherence. If adherence
drops as equity grows (or after a new equity high), the Money Paradox is active.

### Faith's Four Core Principles (p.55, p.258-259)

> "Trade with an edge, manage risk, be consistent, and keep it simple. The entire Turtle
> training, and indeed the basis for all successful trading, can be summed up in these four core
> principles." (p.55)

1. **Trade with an edge** -- positive expectancy, verified by testing.
2. **Manage risk** -- survive long enough for the edge to manifest.
3. **Be consistent** -- execute every signal. The skipped trade kills the expectancy.
4. **Keep it simple** -- complex systems break. Simple systems endure.

## BOOK 3: Reminiscences of a Stock Operator (Livermore/Lefevre, 2006)

### The Sitting Tight Principle (Ch. V, p.35-37)

The single most famous passage in trading literature:

> "After spending many years in Wall Street and after making and losing millions of dollars I
> want to tell you this: It never was my thinking that made the big money for me. It always was
> my sitting. Got that? My sitting tight!" (p.35)

> "Men who can both be right and sit tight are uncommon. I found it one of the hardest things to
> learn. But it is only after a stock operator has firmly grasped this that he can make big money."
> (p.35)

**Named Principle: SITTING TIGHT.** The money is not in the entry or the exit but in the
holding. The psychological difficulty is enduring intermediate adverse moves while the thesis
remains intact. Most traders exit too early because they cannot tolerate unrealized profit
drawdowns.

**Detectable Pattern:** Compare actual hold time to system-prescribed hold time. If actual <
prescribed, the trader is cutting winners. Calculate the P&L difference between actual exits
and system exits.

#### Old Man Partridge (Ch. V, p.36-37)

Livermore describes an old trader at his brokerage named Partridge who had one piece of
advice for everyone:

> "Well, you know, this is a bull market!" (p.36)

People would give Partridge tips to sell specific stocks and re-enter lower. His response was
always the same: he couldn't sell because it was a bull market. He understood that the big move
was more valuable than clever scalping, and that selling meant risking his position.

> "He had the confidence and the patience to wait." (p.37)

**Named Principle: POSITION OVER CLEVERNESS.** Trying to trade around a core position
(selling to buy back lower) is intellectually satisfying but practically destructive. It gives you
many chances to lose your position and only the illusion of optimization.

### The Right-and-Broke Lesson (Ch. VIII, p.55-57)

Livermore identified the 1906 bear market correctly but sold too early and was cleaned out by
rallies before the big break came:

> "There I was -- right and busted!" (p.56)

> "It was the first time that I had seen big money ahead, and I naturally started toward it on the
> run. Before I could reach the dollar-pile my wind went back on me and I fell to the ground."
> (p.56)

> "I should have walked and not sprinted." (p.56)

**Named Principle: RIGHT IDEA, WRONG TIMING.** Being correct about direction is necessary but
not sufficient. Premature entry with full size is as destructive as being wrong. The solution is
scaling in: start small, add as the market confirms.

**Detectable Pattern:** Trades entered at maximum size before confirmation vs. trades that
scale in. Calculate the survival rate and average drawdown of each approach.

### The Revenge Trading Cycle (Ch. III-IV, p.20-25)

Livermore describes multiple cycles of going broke and recovering:

> "I went broke several times, and that is never pleasant, but the way I lost money is the way
> everybody loses money who loses money in Wall Street." (p.20)

> "A man must believe in himself and his judgment if he expects to make a living at this game.
> That is why I don't believe in tips." (p.20)

The pattern: make money, increase lifestyle, trade larger to fund lifestyle, blow up.

**Named Principle: LIFESTYLE CREEP AS RISK FACTOR.** Success breeds spending, spending demands
returns, return pressure drives oversizing. Livermore went broke at least four times, and each
time the proximate cause was trading too large to support an escalated lifestyle.

**Detectable Pattern:** Track whether position sizes increase after winning periods without a
corresponding increase in total equity. Lifestyle-driven sizing shows as risk-per-trade creep
during winning streaks.

### Overtrading After Wins (Ch. V, p.35-36)

> "The more I made the more I spent. This is the usual experience with most men." (p.35)

> "Don't misunderstand me. I never allowed pleasure to interfere with business. When I lost it
> was because I was wrong and not because I was suffering from dissipation or excess." (p.35)

**Named Principle: THE WINNER'S CURSE.** Winning streaks create overconfidence. Overconfidence
increases position size. Increased position size amplifies the next loss. The cycle is:
win -> confidence -> larger size -> larger loss -> fear -> smaller size -> miss the recovery.

**Detectable Pattern:** Position size as a function of recent win/loss streak. If size increases
after 3+ consecutive wins, the Winner's Curse is active.

### Learning from Mistakes (Ch. VIII, p.56)

> "I have always found it profitable to study my mistakes." (p.56)

> "If a man didn't make mistakes he'd own the world in a month. But if he didn't profit by his
> mistakes he wouldn't own a blessed thing." (p.57)

**Named Principle: MISTAKE MINING.** Every loss contains data. The discipline is not just to
review losses but to extract the specific behavioral error vs. the statistical noise. Not every
loss is a mistake -- some are just the cost of playing. The key question is: "Did I follow my
system?"

### The Tip Trap (Ch. XII, p.88-98)

Livermore's extended meditation on why tips destroy traders:

> "I know from experience that nobody can give me a tip or a series of tips that will make more
> money for me than my own judgment." (p.20)

> "A speculator has a host of enemies, many of whom successfully bore from within." (p.88)

> "I am fairly immune from the commoner speculative ailments, such as greed and fear and hope.
> But being an ordinary man I find I can err with great ease." (p.88)

**Named Principle: THE TIP AS PARASITE.** Tips create dependency. If you enter on someone
else's analysis, you have no framework for exiting. You need the tipper to tell you when to
sell, and the tipper is never there when you need them.

**Detectable Pattern:** Trades entered without a predefined stop or exit criteria. These are
tip-driven or impulse trades. They should be flagged and their P&L tracked separately.

### The Patience Principle (Ch. IX-X, p.61-72)

> "There is a time for all things, but I didn't know it. And that is precisely what beats so many
> men in Wall Street who are very far from being in the main sucker class. There is the plain
> fool, who does the wrong thing at all times everywhere, but there is the Wall Street fool, who
> thinks he must trade all the time." (p.61)

**Named Principle: THE COMPULSION TO TRADE.** Most losses come from trading when there is no
signal. Boredom is the most expensive emotion after ego. The market does not owe you a trade
today.

**Detectable Pattern:** Trade frequency analysis. Compare trades taken during valid signal
periods vs. "quiet" periods. If a significant portion of losses come from non-signal trades,
the Compulsion to Trade is active.

> "It takes a man a long time to learn all the lessons of all his mistakes. They say there are two
> sides to everything. But there is only one side to the stock market; and it is not the bull side
> or the bear side, but the right side." (p.61)

### Emotional Recovery (Ch. IV, p.25-34)

After going completely broke in New York:

> "I always knew I would have another chance and that I would not make the same mistake a
> second time. I believed in myself." (p.20)

> "The game taught me the game. And it didn't spare the rod while teaching." (p.20)

**Named Principle: RESILIENT SELF-BELIEF.** There is a critical distinction between confidence
in your system/method and confidence in yourself as a learner. The first can be shaken by
drawdowns; the second is what brings you back. Livermore's self-belief survived total
financial destruction multiple times.

### Position Sizing Discipline (Ch. IX, p.64-65)

> "Never play macho man with the market." (p.63, Jones quoting his own lesson but echoing
> Livermore)

Livermore's pattern across his career: the losses were never from being wrong about direction.
They were from being right about direction but wrong about SIZE.

> "My major problem was not the number of points I lost on the trade, but that I was trading far
> too many contracts relative to the equity in the accounts." (p.63)

**Named Principle: SIZE KILLS.** Direction is binary (right or wrong). But size is continuous,
and wrong size turns right direction into bankruptcy. A correct thesis with 10x leverage is more
dangerous than a wrong thesis with 1x leverage.

## SYNTHESIS: Principles for an AI Trading Coach

### The 12 Named Principles (Priority Order for System Traders)

1. **DEFENSE FIRST** (Jones) -- Think about what you can lose before what you can make.
2. **THE INDIFFERENCE THRESHOLD** (Hite) -- Size positions so individual outcomes don't matter.
3. **BET QUALITY INDEPENDENCE** (Hite) -- Judge trades by process, not outcome.
4. **SITTING TIGHT** (Livermore) -- The money is in the holding, not the trading.
5. **RIGHTNESS IS IRRELEVANT** (Faith) -- Win rate doesn't measure trading quality.
6. **THE SKIPPED TRADE** (Hite/Faith) -- The trade you skip is the one that pays.
7. **SYSTEM-PERSONALITY FIT** (Van Tharp) -- The best system you won't follow is worthless.
8. **EGO SEPARATION** (Schwartz) -- Needing to be right is the most expensive emotion.
9. **PROCESS AMNESIA** (Faith) -- Reset after each trade. Previous outcome is irrelevant.
10. **THE MONEY PARADOX** (Faith) -- Caring about money degrades performance.
11. **SIZE KILLS** (Livermore) -- Wrong position size turns correct direction into bankruptcy.
12. **THE COMPULSION TO TRADE** (Livermore) -- No signal means no trade. Period.

### Drawdown Discipline Protocol (Synthesized from All Three Books)

1. **Before entering any trade:** Know your stop. Know your position size. Know your max loss
   for the period. (Kovner, Jones, Hite)
2. **During a drawdown:** Reduce size. Shift focus from P&L to system adherence. The goal is
   to survive, not to recover quickly. (Schwartz: tactical withdrawal)
3. **After consecutive losses:** Do NOT skip the next signal. Do NOT increase size. The system
   is designed for this. (Faith: 17 consecutive cocoa losses before the big winner)
4. **At the monthly/weekly circuit breaker:** Stop trading. No exceptions. Review journal.
   Identify whether losses were system losses (acceptable) or behavioral losses (fixable).
   (Jones: never a double-digit monthly loss)
5. **Recovery from drawdown:** Scale back in with minimum size. Win back confidence before
   size. Small wins rebuild neural reward pathways. (Schwartz: "pull back, lick my wounds,
   and come back")
6. **Post-recovery:** Resist the urge to "make up for lost time" with oversized positions.
   The Winner's Curse follows every recovery. (Livermore: "the more I made the more I spent")

### The Single Most Important Quote for System Traders

From Larry Hite (p.88):

> "There is a very important message here: People don't change. That is why this whole game
> works."

The edge in systematic trading exists precisely because human psychology is stable and
predictable. Cognitive biases are permanent features of human decision-making. Your system
exploits these biases in others. Your discipline prevents these biases in yourself. The system
works because it is inhuman. Your job is to not re-humanize it.
