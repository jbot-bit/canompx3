# Trading Psychology Frameworks for AI Coaching System

Extracted from three foundational texts. Each concept includes: definition, detectable behavioral patterns (from trade data), intervention strategies, and page references.

## BOOK 1: Trading in the Zone (Mark Douglas, 2000)

### 1.1 The Five Fundamental Truths

Douglas identifies five truths that, when fully integrated into a trader's belief system, eliminate emotional risk and produce consistency. These are the core axioms of the probabilistic mindset.

**Truth 1: Anything can happen.** (p.98-99)
- Definition: No matter how much evidence supports a trade, the outcome is uncertain. Any single trader anywhere in the world can negate your edge.
- Why it matters: If a trader truly believes anything can happen, there is nothing for the mind to avoid or block. It acts as "an expansive force on perception."
- Detectable violation: Increasing position size after a string of wins (overconfidence). Taking excessive risk on "sure thing" setups. Failure to use stops.

**Truth 2: You don't need to know what is going to happen next in order to make money.** (p.99)
- Definition: Consistent profitability comes from edge exploitation over a series, not from predicting individual outcomes.
- Detectable violation: Skipping trades that match the system because "this one doesn't feel right." Hesitation entries (late fills). Adding discretionary filters not in the plan.

**Truth 3: There is a random distribution between wins and losses for any given set of variables that define an edge.** (p.99-100)
- Definition: Wins and losses are randomly distributed within the sample. You cannot know the sequence in advance.
- Detectable violation: Reducing size after 2-3 losses (loss aversion). Increasing size after 2-3 wins (recency bias). Any attempt to "time" when to apply the system.

**Truth 4: An edge is nothing more than an indication of a higher probability of one thing happening over another.** (p.100)
- Definition: An edge does not guarantee outcomes on individual trades. Gathering "other" evidence for or against a trade is as pointless as predicting the next coin flip after 10 tails.
- Detectable violation: Seeking confirming information beyond system parameters. Adding indicators not in the plan. Delaying entry to "wait for confirmation" outside the rules.

**Truth 5: Every moment in the market is unique.** (p.100-101)
- Definition: No two moments produce identical results because the constellation of traders is always different. Similar-looking patterns will produce different results.
- Detectable violation: Refusing to take a trade because "this pattern failed last time." Over-generalizing from recent experience. Revenge trading after a loss on a similar setup.

### 1.2 Probabilistic Thinking Framework

**The Casino Analogy** (p.80-82)
- Definition: Casinos maintain consistent profitability despite random individual outcomes because they (a) have a genuine edge, (b) execute on every qualifying event, (c) think in terms of large sample sizes, and (d) never let individual outcomes affect their behavior.
- The four elements a trader must replicate:
  1. **Genuine statistical edge** (the system)
  2. **Flawless execution** on every signal
  3. **Sample-size thinking** (20+ trades minimum to evaluate)
  4. **Emotional detachment** from individual outcomes
- Detectable violations: Skip rate > 0% on valid signals. Position size variance not explained by system rules. Changing systems after < 20 trades.

**Random Outcome, Consistent Results Paradox** (p.80-83)
- Definition: The outcome of each individual trade is random and unpredictable, yet the net result of a series of trades is statistically reliable and predictable. This is the core paradox most traders cannot accept.
- Key insight: "If you really believe in an uncertain outcome, then you also have to expect that virtually anything can happen. Otherwise, the moment you let your mind hold onto the idea that you know, you stop taking all of the unknown variables into consideration."

### 1.3 Emotional Risk Elimination Model

**What Creates Emotional Risk** (p.92-94)
- Definition: Emotional pain in trading comes from the gap between what you EXPECT and what the market DOES. If you genuinely expect anything can happen, there is nothing the market can do to cause emotional pain.
- The mechanism: Expectations create mental rigidity. The mind filters out information that contradicts expectations. This causes the trader to hold losers (expecting reversal) and cut winners (expecting reversal).
- Douglas's formula: **Emotional Risk = f(Unresolved Expectations)**

**Three Stages of Eliminating Emotional Risk** (p.92-94)
1. Stop expecting specific outcomes from individual trades
2. Stop defining market information as threatening (reframe losses as costs of doing business)
3. Align beliefs with the five fundamental truths

**Detectable behavioral markers of emotional risk:**
- Holding losers past stop (expecting reversal) -- measurable as avg loss > planned risk
- Cutting winners early (expecting reversal) -- measurable as avg win < system target
- Hesitation on entries (fear) -- measurable as entry price slippage beyond normal
- Revenge trading after losses -- measurable as increased frequency after losing trades
- Position size inflation after wins -- measurable as size drift correlated with P&L

### 1.4 The Belief System Model

**How Beliefs Create Trading Reality** (p.102-121)

Douglas models beliefs as energy structures that:
1. **Actively resist change** -- beliefs push back against contradictory information (p.111-113)
2. **Filter perception** -- beliefs determine what information you notice vs. ignore (p.63-66)
3. **Create expectations** -- beliefs auto-generate expectations about what "should" happen (p.88-92)

**Primary Characteristics of a Belief** (p.111-120)
1. Beliefs shape perception (you see what you believe)
2. Beliefs are self-reinforcing (confirming evidence feels "right")
3. Beliefs resist anything that challenges their validity
4. Every belief demands expression (suppressed beliefs create internal conflict)
5. Beliefs continue to function regardless of whether you are conscious of them

**The Losing Trader's Belief Stack** (common dysfunctional beliefs):
- "The market owes me" (entitlement)
- "I was right, the market was wrong" (blame externalization)
- "This time is different" (pattern override)
- "I can't afford to lose" (loss aversion as identity)
- "The next trade will make up for it" (revenge/recovery fantasy)

**Detectable from trade data:**
- Entitlement: Increasing risk after a drawdown period
- Blame externalization: No behavioral change after losses (same mistakes repeated)
- Pattern override: Overriding stops on specific setups
- Loss aversion: Stop widening during trade (moving stops further away)
- Recovery fantasy: Doubling position size after large losses

### 1.5 The Mechanical Stage

**Four-Phase Progression to Consistency** (p.122-141)

**Phase 1: The Mechanical Stage** (p.123-127)
- Definition: Trading a defined edge with absolute mechanical discipline for a minimum of 20 trades ("the exercise"). No discretion. Every signal taken. Exact size. Exact stops.
- Purpose: Build the neural pathways of disciplined execution. Prove to yourself that the edge works.
- Rules of the exercise (p.133-140):
  1. Choose one market
  2. Define your edge with objective variables
  3. Pre-define risk (1R) on every trade
  4. Take EVERY signal without exception
  5. Monitor yourself for errors, not profits
  6. Minimum 20 trades to complete one cycle
  7. If you break a rule, start the 20-trade count over

**Phase 2: The Subjective Stage**
- After mechanical consistency is proven, the trader begins incorporating market-reading skill
- Discretion is EARNED, not assumed

**Phase 3: The Intuitive Stage**
- Pattern recognition operates below conscious awareness
- Trades "feel right" because the belief system is aligned

**The Role of Self-Discipline** (p.127-130)
- Definition: Self-discipline is a *technique* for redirecting attention from a conflicting belief to a desired intention. It is NOT willpower.
- Key insight: "Self-discipline is not a personality trait... it is a technique to create a new mental framework."
- It is only needed during the Mechanical Stage. Once the new beliefs are installed, the desired behavior becomes automatic.

### 1.6 The Attitude Survey (p.141-144)

Douglas provides a self-assessment tool. Key diagnostic questions (paraphrased for coaching system):

| Question Domain | What It Measures |
|----------------|-----------------|
| "Do you monitor your emotions during a trade?" | Self-awareness |
| "Do you consider yourself responsible for every outcome?" | Locus of control |
| "Do you pre-define risk before every trade?" | Planning discipline |
| "Do you take every signal your system generates?" | Execution discipline |
| "After a loss, do you feel compelled to make it back immediately?" | Revenge tendency |
| "Do you feel euphoria after a win?" | Emotional attachment |
| "Do you hesitate on entries?" | Fear response |
| "Do you add to losing positions?" | Hope/denial |

### 1.7 Detectable Pattern Taxonomy (Douglas)

| Pattern | Trade Data Signal | Underlying Belief Violation |
|---------|------------------|-----------------------------|
| **Cherry-picking** | Skip rate on valid signals | Truth 2 (need to know) |
| **Revenge trading** | Trade frequency spike after losses | Truth 1 (anything can happen) |
| **Stop widening** | Actual loss > planned risk | Truth 3 (random distribution) |
| **Winner cutting** | Avg win << system expectancy | Truth 5 (every moment unique) |
| **Size escalation** | Position size drift after wins | Truth 4 (edge = probability) |
| **Paralysis** | Missed signals after drawdown | Truth 1 (anything can happen) |
| **Overtrading** | Trades outside system parameters | Addiction to random rewards (p.35) |
| **System hopping** | < 20 trades before changing rules | Failure to think in probabilities |

## BOOK 2: New Trading for a Living (Alexander Elder, 2014)

### 2.1 The Three M's Framework

Elder's master framework: **Mind, Method, Money** (p.19-20)
- **Mind** = Individual psychology and discipline
- **Method** = Technical analysis and trading system
- **Money** = Risk management (position sizing, 2%/6% rules)

All three must be present. A deficiency in any one kills the trader.

### 2.2 Individual Psychology (Part 1, Chapters 4-10)

**The AA Analogy** (p.37-42)
- Definition: Elder, a psychiatrist, models trading addiction/compulsion using Alcoholics Anonymous principles.
- Key mapping:

| AA Concept | Trading Equivalent | Page |
|-----------|-------------------|------|
| **Denial** | "I don't have a problem, I just need a better system" | p.37 |
| **Rock bottom** | Account blown, relationships damaged, can't stop | p.38, 40 |
| **First step** | Admitting "I am a losing trader" / "I am powerless over the market" | p.38, 41 |
| **One day at a time** | Focus on today's trade, not recovering past losses | p.38 |
| **Meeting** | Daily self-review: "What did I do well? What can I improve?" | p.39, 42 |

**Trader's Rock Bottom** (p.40)
- Not just financial: it's the moment a trader admits that something in their BEHAVIOR, not the market, is causing losses.
- Detectable: Drawdown > 25% of peak equity + repeated identical mistakes in trade journal.

**The Losers Anonymous Protocol** (p.39-42)
1. Admit you are a losing trader (not "temporarily unlucky")
2. Identify your specific pattern of self-destruction
3. Commit to one day at a time: today's trade only, no mental accounting of past losses
4. Hold a "meeting for one" every evening: review trades, grade discipline, not P&L
5. Find a support structure (trading group, mentor, or AI coach)

**Self-Destructiveness Taxonomy** (p.32-35)
Elder identifies three modes of self-destruction:

| Mode | Definition | Detectable Pattern |
|------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Gambling** | Trading for excitement, not profit. Action addiction. | High frequency + no edge. Many trades outside system. |
| **Self-sabotage** | Unconscious desire to lose. Often linked to guilt about profits or fear of success. | Consistently good analysis + consistently bad execution. Knows the right action but does the opposite. |
| **Demolition derby** | Deliberate account destruction during emotional crisis. | Massive position sizing spike. Removal of all stops. "Blow up" trades. |

**Controlling Self-Destructiveness** (p.35)
- Keep a diary (not just trade log -- emotional state diary)
- Set hard loss limits (2% rule, 6% rule -- see below)
- "A meeting for one" daily
- External accountability (another person or system that monitors)

**Emotional Trading Classification** (p.44-45)
Elder classifies emotional states that corrupt trading:

| Emotional State | Behavioral Marker | Market Phase |
|----------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Euphoria** | Over-sizing, removing stops | After a winning streak |
| **Fear** | Paralysis, missed entries, premature exits | After a losing streak |
| **Hope** | Holding losers, widening stops | During a drawdown |
| **Greed** | Adding to winners recklessly, ignoring exits | During a strong trend |
| **Despair** | Revenge trading, quadrupling down | After significant loss |

**Bending the Rules** (p.35-36)
- Key insight: "A social drinker can have a few drinks and stop, but an alcoholic cannot stop after the first drink. Similarly, a losing trader cannot bend one rule."
- Detection: ANY deviation from the plan. One bent rule predicts more. The first exception is the diagnostic signal.
- Intervention: Zero-tolerance policy. If a rule is bent, halt trading for the remainder of the session.

### 2.3 The 2% Rule (p.219-223)

**Definition:** Never risk more than 2% of your trading account on any single trade.

**Formula:**
```
Max position risk = Account equity x 0.02
Number of shares/contracts = Max position risk / (Entry price - Stop price)
```

**The Iron Triangle of Risk Control** (p.220-221)
Three variables, pick any two:
1. Account size
2. Risk per trade (distance to stop in dollars)
3. Position size (number of contracts)

If you fix account size and define your stop, position size is determined. No discretion.

**Detectable violations:**
- Any trade risking > 2% of account at entry
- Stop distance that implies > 2% risk at given size
- Position size inconsistent with stated stop level

**Psychological function:** The 2% rule makes individual losses insignificant. When a single loss cannot meaningfully damage you, fear diminishes. Fear diminishes, execution improves.

### 2.4 The 6% Rule (p.224-228)

**Definition:** If your total open risk (sum of all positions' risk-to-stop) plus closed losses for the current month reaches 6% of beginning-of-month equity, STOP TRADING for the rest of the month.

**The Concept of Available Risk** (p.224-228)
```
Available risk = 6% of month-start equity - (closed losses this month + open risk on current positions)
```
- If available risk = 0 or negative: no new trades allowed
- The 6% rule is a CIRCUIT BREAKER -- it forces a pause during drawdowns

**Detectable from trade data:**
- Monthly loss approaching 6% -- warning signal
- Continuing to trade after 6% threshold -- violation
- Acceleration of trade frequency when approaching limit -- desperation signal

**Psychological function:** Prevents the "one more trade to get it back" spiral. Forces cooling-off period. Protects account from demolition-derby behavior.

### 2.5 Trade Grading System (p.226-228, 238-243)

**Buy Grade** (p.226):
```
Buy grade = (high - buy_point) / (high - low)
```
Where high and low are for the entry bar. Measures how close to the low of the bar you entered. Above 50% = good entry.

**Sell Grade** (p.226):
```
Sell grade = (sell_point - low) / (high - low)
```
Measures how close to the high of the bar you exited. Above 50% = good exit.

**Trade Apgar Score** (p.238-243)
- Named after Virginia Apgar's newborn health scoring system
- Rates trades on a structured scale immediately after entry
- Components score: entry quality, stop placement, position sizing adherence, setup quality, timing

**A-Trade Classification** (p.228-230)
- Before every trade, ask: "Is this an A-trade?" (a perfect setup for YOUR system)
- Only A-trades get executed
- Detectable: Track what percentage of trades match predefined A-trade criteria
- If non-A-trade percentage > 20%, discipline is degrading

### 2.6 The Trading Diary Framework (p.245-247)

Elder's diary is NOT just a trade log. It has three layers:

| Layer | Content | Review Frequency |
|-------|---------|-----------------|
| **Trade plan** | Entry, target, stop, size calculation, A-trade check | Before entry |
| **Emotional state** | How you feel, confidence level, any urges | During trade |
| **Post-trade review** | What happened, buy/sell grades, what you'd do differently | After exit |

**Three Benefits of the Trade Journal** (p.245):
1. **Immediate: Sense of order** -- Writing the plan prevents impulsive trading
2. **Short-term: Trade review** -- Examining closed trades at the chart's "hard right edge" reveals decision quality
3. **Long-term: Equity curve analysis** -- Separate equity curves by strategy, by long/short, by exit type, by emotional state

**Key insight (p.247):** "Once you see an equity curve for exits marked 'Couldn't stand the pain,' you'll never trade without stops!"

### 2.7 The Businessman's Risk Model (p.201-202)

**Definition:** A good trade is one where you risk a relatively small amount to test a hypothesis. If it works, you profit. If not, the loss is small and predefined -- like a businessman investing in inventory.

**Contrast with gambling:** A gambler makes impulsive bets based on hope. A businessman calculates risk/reward before committing capital.

**Mathematical expectation** (p.200-201):
```
Expectation = (avg_win x win_rate) - (avg_loss x loss_rate)
```
Must be positive BEFORE placing any trade. If you cannot calculate this for your system, you are gambling.

### 2.8 Elder's Discipline Taxonomy

| Discipline Rule | Detection Method | Intervention |
|----------------|-----------------|--------------|
| Never trade without a plan | Trades with no pre-filed entry/stop/target | Block trade execution |
| Never risk > 2% per trade | Position size vs. stop distance vs. account | Auto-calculate max size |
| Stop when 6% monthly loss hit | Running monthly P&L tracking | Halt signal |
| Grade every trade | Missing grades in journal | Prompt for grades |
| Hold "meeting for one" daily | No end-of-day review | Evening prompt |
| No rule-bending | ANY plan deviation | Session halt |
| A-trades only | Non-A-trade percentage | Warn when > 20% |

## BOOK 3: Trade Your Way to Financial Freedom (Van K. Tharp, 2006)

### 3.1 The Holy Grail is YOU (Chapter 1, p.3-14)

**Core thesis:** The "Holy Grail" of trading is not a system -- it is self-knowledge. The most important factor in trading success is the psychology of the trader, not the method.

**What's Really Important to Trading** (p.9-11):
1. The trading system (10% of success)
2. Money management / position sizing (30% of success)
3. **Psychology (60% of success)**

Van Tharp's hierarchy: You --> Objectives --> Position Sizing --> System. Most traders invert this, obsessing over the system.

### 3.2 Judgmental Biases Taxonomy (Chapter 2, p.17-41)

Van Tharp catalogs cognitive biases organized by WHERE they corrupt the trading process.

**Biases Affecting System Development** (p.20-35):

| Bias | Definition | Detection |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| **Representation bias** | Assuming a small sample represents the population | Changing system after < 30 trades |
| **Reliability bias** | Trusting data/sources without verification | Not auditing system backtest |
| **Lotto bias** | Wanting to be right rather than make money | High win rate obsession, cutting winners |
| **Law of small numbers** | Drawing conclusions from too few observations | Strategy evaluation on < 30 trades |
| **Conservatism bias** | Failing to update beliefs with new evidence | Ignoring system signals that contradict prior view |
| **Randomness bias** | Seeing patterns in random data | "The market always does X on Fridays" |
| **Need-to-understand bias** | Requiring a narrative for every market move | Over-analyzing losses, adding indicators |

**Biases Affecting System Testing** (p.35-38):

| Bias | Definition | Detection |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| **Degrees-of-freedom bias** | Over-optimizing parameters to fit historical data | System has > 5 free parameters |
| **Postdictive error** | Using information not available at trade time | Indicators calculated on future data |
| **Not-enough-data bias** | Testing on too little data to be meaningful | < 100 trades in backtest |
| **Failure-to-test bias** | Trading a system without testing it first | No documented backtest results |

**Biases Affecting Live Trading** (p.38-41):

| Bias | Definition | Detection |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| **Gambler's fallacy** | "I'm due for a win after 3 losses" | Size increase after losing streak |
| **Conservative-with-profits bias** | Taking small profits, letting losses run | Avg win << avg loss in R-multiples |
| **My-current-trade bias** | Over-attending to open positions | Micro-managing, adjusting stops intraday |
| **Sunk-cost bias** | "I've already lost $X, might as well hold" | Holding past stop because of accumulated loss |

### 3.3 The R-Multiple Framework (Chapter 6, p.143-155)

**Definition:** R = the initial risk on a trade (distance from entry to stop, in dollars). All outcomes are expressed as multiples of R.

| Outcome | R-Multiple | Meaning |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Full loss | -1R | Lost exactly what was risked |
| Half loss | -0.5R | Lost half of planned risk |
| Breakeven | 0R | No gain, no loss |
| Target hit at 2:1 | +2R | Made twice what was risked |
| Big winner | +5R | Made five times what was risked |
| Disaster | -3R | Lost three times planned risk (stop failure) |

**Why R-multiples matter psychologically:**
- They normalize all trades to the same scale regardless of instrument, size, or market
- They make it impossible to hide from the truth: if avg R is negative, you are losing
- They separate "being right" from "making money" -- you can be wrong 60% of the time and still profit if winners are large enough in R-terms

**Detectable from trade data:**
- R-multiple distribution: Is it positively skewed? (Big winners, small losers)
- Percentage of trades > -1R: How often do you exceed planned risk?
- Largest negative R: How bad is the worst-case execution?
- Mean R vs. system expectancy: Are you capturing what the system offers?

### 3.4 Expectancy (Chapter 6, p.137-155)

**Definition:**
```
Expectancy = (% wins x avg win in R) + (% losses x avg loss in R)
```

Example: 40% win rate, avg win = +2.5R, avg loss = -1R
```
Expectancy = (0.40 x 2.5) + (0.60 x -1.0) = 1.0 - 0.6 = +0.40R per trade
```

**The Snow Fight Metaphor** (p.133-137)
- Imagine a snowball fight where:
  - For every snowball that hits you, you pay $1 (losses)
  - For every snowball you throw that hits, you get $2 (wins)
  - You hit 40% of the time, they hit 60%
- Most people focus on "I'm getting hit more than I'm hitting" (the win rate)
- Smart traders focus on: "I make $0.80 per round on average, so I want to play as many rounds as possible"
- **Lesson: Win rate is psychologically seductive but economically secondary to expectancy**

**Detectable coaching moments:**
- Trader obsessing over win rate instead of expectancy
- Trader refusing systems with < 50% win rate
- Trader cutting winners to maintain high win rate (sacrificing expectancy)

### 3.5 Position Sizing Psychology (Chapter 12, p.151-180)

**The Ralph Vince Experiment** (p.151-152, originally p.38)
- 40 PhDs played a game with 60% win probability, 1:1 payout
- Each started with $1,000, played 100 rounds
- **95% of them lost money** despite the positive expectancy
- Cause: Bad position sizing driven by psychology (gambler's fallacy, size escalation after losses)

**Position Sizing as "How Much"** (p.151-155)
- Van Tharp reframes position sizing as THE critical decision, not entry timing
- "Position sizing is the part of your trading system that tells you HOW MUCH"
- Most traders spend 90% of effort on WHEN to enter and 10% on HOW MUCH -- should be reversed

**Anti-Martingale vs. Martingale** (p.155-165)

| Strategy | Definition | Psychology |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| **Martingale** | Increase size after losses | "I'm due" -- gambler's fallacy. Guarantees ruin. |
| **Anti-Martingale** | Increase size after wins (percent-risk model) | "Let profits compound" -- mathematically sound. |

**Percent Risk Model** (p.165-168)
```
Position size = (Account x Risk%) / R_per_unit
```
- Risk% = percentage of account risked per trade (typically 0.5-2%)
- R_per_unit = dollar risk per share/contract (entry - stop)
- This IS the Van Tharp version of Elder's 2% rule

**Detectable position sizing pathologies:**
- **Martingale behavior**: Size increases after losses
- **Random sizing**: No consistent relationship between account size and position size
- **Fixed-size ignoring account**: Same number of contracts regardless of account growth/decline
- **Ruin trajectory**: Current sizing + current loss rate = eventual ruin (calculate explicitly)

### 3.6 Trader Type Classification (Chapter 3, p.45-57)

Van Tharp identifies trader types by objectives and temperament:

| Trader Type | Time Frame | Key Psychological Challenge |
|-------------|-----------|---------------------------|
| **Scalper/Day Trader** | Minutes-hours | Overtrading, action addiction, fatigue |
| **Swing Trader** | Days-weeks | Overnight anxiety, premature exit on gaps |
| **Position Trader** | Weeks-months | Boredom, "do something" impulse, giving back profits |
| **Long-Term Investor** | Months-years | Conviction during drawdowns, not monitoring enough |

**Tom Basso's Objectives Framework** (p.48-57):
1. What is your annual return target?
2. What maximum drawdown can you tolerate (emotionally, not just financially)?
3. What is your time commitment?
4. How much monitoring can you handle without over-managing?

**Key insight:** The system should match the trader's temperament, not the other way around. A trader who cannot handle overnight risk should NOT trade a swing system, regardless of its edge.

**Detectable mismatches:**
- Position trader checking prices every 5 minutes (over-monitoring)
- Day trader holding overnight (time frame drift)
- Low-drawdown-tolerance trader using high-volatility system (emotional mismatch)

### 3.7 Mental Strategies for Traders (Chapter 1, p.11-14; Chapter 3, p.57)

Van Tharp, drawing from NLP (Neuro-Linguistic Programming), identifies mental strategies used by top traders:

**Modeling Market Geniuses** (p.11-14)
- Top traders share these mental strategies:
  1. **They focus on what they will do if the trade goes WRONG, not what happens if it goes right**
  2. They have low emotional reactivity to individual outcomes
  3. They think in terms of expectancy and R-multiples
  4. They view losses as tuition/business expenses
  5. They have a clear self-concept as "I am a risk manager" not "I am a predictor"

**Pre-Trade Mental Rehearsal** (referenced in Basso's methodology, p.48-52)
- Before each trading day, mentally rehearse:
  1. The worst-case scenario (stop hit, full R loss)
  2. Your response to that scenario (accept it, move on)
  3. The discipline of execution (take every signal)
- This pre-commitment reduces emotional reactivity during live trading

### 3.8 The Six Keys to Investment Success (Chapter 6, p.130-133)

| Key | Definition | Psychological Trap |
|-----|-----------|-------------------|
| 1. **Reliability** | Win rate | Overvalued. Traders sacrifice everything for high win rate. |
| 2. **Relative size of profits to losses** | Avg win / avg loss ratio | Undervalued. The real driver of profitability. |
| 3. **Cost of trading** | Commissions + slippage | Ignored until it compounds. |
| 4. **Opportunity** | How often you trade | Overtrading destroys edge through costs. |
| 5. **Capital** | Account size | Undercapitalization = excessive risk per trade. |
| 6. **Position sizing** | How much per trade | THE most important and most neglected factor. |

### 3.9 Worst-Case Scenario Planning (Chapter 4, p.78)

**Definition:** Before trading any system, explicitly calculate and accept the worst-case scenario.

Procedure:
1. What is the maximum consecutive loss streak the system has produced in testing?
2. Multiply that by 1.5x (it WILL be worse in live trading)
3. Calculate the account impact at your planned position size
4. If you cannot emotionally tolerate that drawdown, reduce size until you can
5. Write this number down and review it weekly

**Detectable violation:** Trader experiences drawdown within normal system parameters but changes the system (proving they had not truly accepted the worst case).

## CROSS-BOOK SYNTHESIS: Encodable Frameworks for AI Coaching

### S1: Behavioral Detection Engine

The three books converge on measurable behavioral patterns. An AI coaching system can detect these from trade data alone:

| Behavior | Douglas Signal | Elder Signal | Van Tharp Signal | Data Required |
|----------|---------------|-------------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Revenge trading** | Trade frequency spike post-loss | "Demolition derby" | Martingale sizing | Timestamps, P&L sequence |
| **Fear/Paralysis** | Skipped signals post-drawdown | "Emotional trading: fear" | Representation bias | Signal log vs. execution log |
| **Overconfidence** | Size escalation post-wins | "Euphoria" | Gambler's fallacy inverse | Size vs. recent P&L |
| **Loss aversion** | Stop widening, holding losers | "Hope" | Conservative-with-profits | Planned stop vs. actual exit |
| **Perfectionism** | Cherry-picking signals | Not taking A-trades | Need-to-understand | Skip rate |
| **Action addiction** | Trades outside system | "Gambling" self-destruction | Overtrading | Trade count vs. signal count |
| **Undisciplined exits** | Cutting winners | "Not being able to sell" | Lotto bias | Avg win vs. system expectancy |
| **Ruin behavior** | N/A | 6% rule violation | Account-destroying sizing | Monthly loss trajectory |

### S2: Intervention Protocol

Mapped by severity level:

| Level | Trigger | Intervention | Source |
|-------|---------|-------------|--------|
| **GREEN** (informational) | Mild deviation from plan (<10% skip rate) | Evening journal prompt, reinforce truths | Douglas p.133 |
| **YELLOW** (warning) | Moderate deviation (avg loss > 1.2R, win cutting) | Pre-trade rehearsal prompt, review R-distribution | Van Tharp p.143, Elder p.226 |

### S3: Structured Self-Assessment Dimensions

Combining all three authors' diagnostic frameworks:

| Dimension | What It Measures | Source |
|-----------|-----------------|--------|
| **Execution fidelity** | % of signals taken exactly as planned | Douglas Mechanical Stage |
| **Risk discipline** | % of trades within 2% rule, avg R-loss | Elder 2%/6%, Van Tharp R-multiples |
| **Emotional neutrality** | Size variance unexplained by system, frequency spikes | Douglas Five Truths, Elder emotional states |
| **Win/loss management** | Avg win R vs. avg loss R, hold time distribution | Van Tharp expectancy, Elder trade grades |
| **Self-awareness** | Journal completion, emotional state logging | Elder diary, Douglas attitude survey |
| **System adherence** | Trades outside plan / total trades | Douglas cherry-picking, Elder A-trades |
| **Recovery behavior** | Post-loss behavioral changes | All three: revenge, paralysis, or disciplined continuation |

### S4: Classification Taxonomies for Coaching Personas

**By Primary Dysfunction (Elder's model):**
1. **The Gambler** -- Trades for excitement. High frequency, no edge. Needs: Boredom tolerance training.
2. **The Self-Saboteur** -- Good analysis, bad execution. Needs: Mechanical stage (Douglas).
3. **The Demolisher** -- Periodic blowups. Needs: Hard circuit breakers (6% rule).

**By Cognitive Bias (Van Tharp's model):**
1. **The Perfectionist** -- Needs to understand everything before acting. Misses trades. Needs: Truth 2 (don't need to know).
2. **The Gambler's Fallacist** -- "I'm due." Sizes up after losses. Needs: Truth 3 (random distribution).
3. **The Win-Rate Addict** -- Cuts winners, holds losers. Needs: R-multiple reframing.
4. **The System Hopper** -- Abandons after < 20 trades. Needs: Mechanical stage commitment.

**By Fear Response (Douglas's model):**
1. **The Hesitator** -- Knows the signal, cannot pull the trigger. Needs: Pre-commitment protocols.
2. **The Micro-Manager** -- Cannot let a trade work. Needs: Truth 5 (every moment is unique).
3. **The Denier** -- Holds losers, blames the market. Needs: Truth 1 (anything can happen).
4. **The Euphoric** -- Over-trades after wins. Needs: Truth 3 (random distribution).
